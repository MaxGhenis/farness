# Referee 1 Report

## Recommendation
Major revision

## Summary for editor
This is a thoughtful and potentially interesting methods paper. The core idea, stability-under-probing, is original enough to merit attention, and the paper is honest about several limitations. However, the current manuscript does not yet establish that the dependent variable is a valid proxy for decision quality rather than a measure of prompt-specific anchoring or probe alignment, and the hierarchical data structure is not yet handled in a way that fully supports the breadth of the claims.

My main reservation is not the existence of an effect, but its interpretation. The paper convincingly shows that farness behaves differently from naive and CoT prompting on the specific probes used here, but it does not yet show that smaller updates imply better decisions, or that the result generalizes beyond this aligned probe set. I would therefore recommend revision focused on narrowing the claims and strengthening the validation design.

## Major concerns

1. **The outcome is not yet a validated proxy for decision quality.** The paper’s central logic is that smaller post-probe updates indicate more stable or more “front-loaded” decision making, but this is still an inference rather than an established construct validity result. The manuscript itself acknowledges that smaller updates may simply reflect greater stubbornness and that better actual decisions cannot be inferred without ground truth ([lines 21-23](../../paper/index.qmd#L21), [lines 300-302](../../paper/index.qmd#L300)). As written, the paper sometimes slides from “stability under probing” to “better decision support” or “measurable analytical value,” but the former does not yet justify the latter.

2. **Prompt-probe alignment is a serious confound that the paper does not yet neutralize.** The farness prompt explicitly instructs the model to consider base rates and identify cognitive biases ([lines 27-36](../../paper/index.qmd#L27)), while the probes then test exactly those dimensions ([lines 147-153](../../paper/index.qmd#L147)). That means the framework is being evaluated on dimensions it was directly told to prioritize, which weakens the causal interpretation of the stability effect. The discussion acknowledges this alignment problem ([lines 280-302](../../paper/index.qmd#L280)), but the paper still treats the resulting effect as evidence that the framework is doing something substantively better rather than simply matching the test. A stronger design would include off-framework probes that target considerations not named in the prompt.

3. **The hierarchical structure of the data is underused in the inferential framing.** The manuscript correctly notes that the 66 observations per cell are repeated stochastic samples nested within 11 scenarios, with effective between-scenario generalization closer to 11 than 66 ([lines 163-165](../../paper/index.qmd#L163)). That point should be taken more seriously in the power statement and in the primary inferential language. The paper still reports a 95% power calculation under independence assumptions and then presents pooled bootstrap CIs and Mann-Whitney tests on the repeated draws ([lines 155-161](../../paper/index.qmd#L155), [lines 202-217](../../paper/index.qmd#L202)). A reader can reasonably ask whether the main uncertainty is at the scenario level, not the run level, and whether the analyses should therefore be framed primarily at the cluster level.

4. **The mixed-effects analysis helps, but it does not fully resolve the dependence and heterogeneity issues.** The random-intercept model is the right direction, but with only 11 scenario groups and very different scales across scenarios, I would want more evidence that the estimates are stable to alternative specifications. In particular, the GPT coefficient is explicitly described as inflated by the leads-scale sycophancy scenario ([lines 198-200](../../paper/index.qmd#L198)), which underscores how sensitive the raw coefficients are to scale and scenario composition. A leave-one-scenario-out analysis, random-slope sensitivity check, or cluster bootstrap would make the inference much more convincing. Right now, the manuscript uses the mixed model as a reason to elevate the farness effect, but the model does not address the core proxy-validity issue and may still overstate certainty about the magnitude of the effect.

5. **The manuscript still overclaims relative to the evidence base.** The abstract and conclusion now read more cautiously than before, but they still emphasize that the method “detects a consistent separation” and that farness yields “more stable estimates” across two frontier models ([lines 9-13](../../paper/index.qmd#L9), [lines 314-318](../../paper/index.qmd#L314)). That is defensible as a case study, but not as a general validation of the framework. The paper would be stronger if it explicitly separated the methodological claim (“this is a usable evaluation strategy”) from the substantive claim (“farness is a superior framework”), and if the latter were made much more provisional.

## Minor comments

- The paper is clearer now that it is framed as a methods paper with farness as a case study ([line 23](../../paper/index.qmd#L23)), but that framing should be carried through more consistently in the title, abstract, and conclusion.
- The worked example at [lines 266-274](../../paper/index.qmd#L266) is helpful, but the new protocol figure at [lines 88-92](../../paper/index.qmd#L88) probably does most of the explanatory work. I would shorten the worked example or move some of its detail to an appendix.
- The 100% initial CI rate is correctly labeled as a prompt-design artifact ([lines 188-192](../../paper/index.qmd#L188)), but it still appears in the main table. If the metric is non-informative by design, it could be demoted in prominence.
- The post hoc downward sycophancy scenario is interesting, but because it is explicitly excluded from the primary analysis ([lines 188-190](../../paper/index.qmd#L188)), the manuscript should keep its exploratory status visually and textually obvious.
- The paper would benefit from one short paragraph stating explicitly what would falsify the interpretation that farness improves decision quality rather than merely producing smaller revisions.

## Decision-contingent revision request

If the authors want this paper to read as a validation of farness, I would need at minimum a sensitivity analysis at the scenario level and an off-framework probe set that reduces alignment with the farness prompt. If the authors instead present the paper as a proof-of-concept methods paper, then the main revision is to narrow the claims and make the construct-validity limits explicit throughout. In that form, I think the paper could become a solid JDM submission.
